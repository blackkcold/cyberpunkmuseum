introduction

This website, Cyberpunk Orientalism Research Centre or CORC ( below), focuses on the presentation of cyberpunk, Orientalism and techno-orientalism images, inspired by my previous research. There is a lack of scholarly and public understanding of the cultural transmission and image construction of this popular culture, especially in terms of relevant research (Roh, Huang and Niu, 2015).

This website will provide as complete a path as possible to show how Orientalism has transformed within the cyberpunk cultural genre, and how it has been inherited and changed into Techno-Orientalism.

what is cyberpunk

Since the science fiction movement in the 1980s, Cyberpunk has been recognized and prevalent in the public as a distinct genre with a unique aesthetic. With outstanding leaders such as William Gibson and Bruce Sterling, and the synchronous prevalence of computer technology and the internet, Cyberpunk has continued its significant influence on modern literature, aesthetic, and culture in areas such as anime, film, games and design (Tatsumi, 2018). Cyberpunk convergent culture and concern with technology has become familiar to the global audience and the transcultural characteristics and dynamic meaning of Cyberpunk are embedded in the process.

what is orientalism

During the research into Cyberpunk, it was found that in the sphere of influential media contents, Cyberpunk has polarized or imbalanced the gaps between the East and the West, with the representative characteristics of Orientalism and a new framework for the new period of techno-orientalism (Sanders, 2008). Thus, Roh, Huang and Niu (2015) mentioned that, in the mid-1990s, Morley and Robins, Ueno, and Sato contributed critiques and views to the freshly conceived ‘techno-orientalism’.

However, in fact, Cyberpunk films not only include the east, but also merge the oriental elements into the narrative and scenes of films, in which part of these eastern images has shaped the orientalism of Cyberpunk (Park, 2004). The Cyberpunk productions of the US have built a high-tech Japan in the background of story, which is pointed out as a ‘default setting for the future’, constructing a techno-Japanism (Taillandier, 2018). Posadas (2017) suggests that ‘Japan’, in other words, is reconstructed in a process of being over coded and determined in the repeating shape of the narrative, which has led to the imagined Japanese images and imagined position of it into a complicated and critical place. Not only the rebuilt Japanese images, but the distortion of these images have also extended into the larger term of ‘Asia’, which provides times-associated dynamic eastern images within a multiple speculative vision of the imaged future (Roh, Huang and Niu, 2015). Stephen Hong Sohn (cited in Fan, 2015) calls this, the ‘re-articulation and re-emergence of the yellow peril’ and it has appeared as a historical variation in the more modern techno-orientalism.

the review of Orientalism and techno-orientalism in cyberpunk in different periods can help distinguish the different characteristics of the changes in techno-orientalism and support the study of the inevitable changes in east-West relations brought about by the economic rise of Asia. In this case, Cyberpunk is not only constructing a current world extension via textual or visual form, but it also presents an aesthetic were eastern atmosphere and orientalism takes on an important position, which is uncommon in other types of production (Fan, 2015).
